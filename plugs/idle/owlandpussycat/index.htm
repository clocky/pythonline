The Quite Remarkable Adventures of The Owl and the Pussycat

In the fall Dove Kids will publish Eric Idle's story "The Quite Remarkable Adventures of the Owl and The Pussycat," based on the famous Edward Lear poem.

The Owl and the Pussycat, illustrated by Edward Lear. (1846)

Here in a shameless attempt to induce you to order the book, the author writes about his admiration for Edward Lear and his art.

Edward Lear and his art.

Most people first encounter Edward Lear through his limericks and nonsense poems. What makes them so peculiar are the wonderfully strange characters with which he illustrates them. I find I am enchanted by the creatures he creates. This for example is his drawing of the Yonghy-Bhongy Bo.



But who could resist "The Scroobius Snake, who always wore a Hat on his Head for fear he should bite anybody?"

or the "Bountiful Beetle, who always carried a Green Umbrella when it didn't rain, and left it at home when it did."

I was familiar with this quirky illustrator side of Lear, since I once made a bathtub out of tiles a friend made from these drawings, but it was some surprise to me when about fifteen years ago the Royal Academy held a retrospective of his paintings. Here were massive Victorian landscapes of great beauty and detail, a world away from his pen and ink illustrations. I was fortunate enough to be guided round this exhibition by the erudite George Melly, himself no stranger to nonsense.


Lear was a quaint old bird who began by painting brilliantly detailed pictures of birds (his owls are spectacular) and for a short while he even instructed Queen Victoria in painting.


He spent most of his life abroad, traveling extensively on painting tours through the Balkans and the Middle East. He was obscure and poor and would probably have remained so but for the publication of A Book of Nonsense in 1846 which was an overnight sensation.

This collection of limericks, and children's poems and nonsense pictures became a best seller (and has ever since remained so.) In this collection was the famous poem The Owl and the Pussycat.

The Owl and the Pussy-cat went to sea

In a beautiful pea-green boat,

They took some honey, and plenty of money,

Wrapped up in a five-pound note.

The Owl looked up to the stars above,

And sang to a small guitar,

"O lovely Pussy! O Pussy, my love,

What a beautiful Pussy you are,

You are,

You are!

What a beautiful Pussy you are!"

In 1993 John Du Prez and I were casting around for a subject for an animated musical. We had written two musicals together, one of which (Behind The Crease) had been produced on BBC Radio. One day I found myself reciting to my daughter Lily the poem of The Owl and The Pussycat. Like most children she fell under the spell of its charm. I realized that its fabulous combination of love story, adventure and happy resolution would make a great musical subject.

John and I began by writing some songs. I like to do this in a new project since when you get stuck on the writing the songs can inspire you and encourage you to continue. So we sat in an old stone cabin in Provence, John with his keyboard and me with guitar and bashed out about twenty songs,

which we stuck in some kind of order on battered cork boards. I then began the writing task. This continued off and on for three years.

Since in the beginning we were writing a musical animated film we were working on a three act structure. The poem itself begins bang at the start of Act Two with Owl and Pussycat running away to sea. There is no explanation of who they are or how they meet (a bird and a cat??) or why they do such a bizarre thing as putting to sea with only some honey and plenty of money wrapped up in a five pound note. This left Act One very much open. It was my job to construct the back story, to create a reason for them to sail for a year and a day to the land where the Bong Tree grows.


At first I wrote a very Dickensian opening with a timorous Owl, recently orphaned by the death of his Uncle, sent off to an unpleasant boarding school called Old Wolves Hall, where a voracious Mr. Wolf and his unpleasant wife Virginia cowed the boys until Owl stood up to him defied him and ran away to sea. There was something about a Pirate chest in the attic and a sinister Captain Cat returning looking for his map to find "the land where the Bong Tree grows." This was all too obviously a lift from Treasure Island combined with Nicholas Nickelby and though much of this early plot was written we abandoned it (or it abandoned we.)

For a while we suffered the disadvantages of encouragement from a Major Studio, which entailed receiving notes from an Executive (who we will call Ann Executive in order to spare his blushes.) Often such notes are the writers equivalent of an enema, they induce shit, and after a few frustrating months of attempting to satisfy the whims of Ann Executive John Du Prez carefully reminded me that he was talking through his fundament, and not even paying for the privilege, and we sailed off into the wonderful waters of the State of Development.

I had a pleasant meeting with a Mr. Steven Spielberg, who listened politely to my pitch (with a streaming cold) and kindly declined because he said "Animations with animal leads don't make money." I was encouraged to discover that even the greatest of names can be mistaken when The Lion King opened a few weeks later! He then cast me as Dibbs in Casper to prevent my writing anything further…



But I am not easily dissuaded by sense and John Du Prez kept digging, prodding and encouraging me and gradually a shape began to emerge. I was constantly inspired by Lear himself, there are some wonderfully silly prose pieces as well as the Nonsense poems and I particularly enjoyed his silly botanical drawings. Who for example could resist a botanist who created this plant?I love the way Lear deals with these plants as though they were real, even down to the Latin names. This for example he calls The Many Peeplia Upsidedownia . Or how about the strangely apt

Piggy-wiggia.Or a flower which grows guitars?

All these themes (guitars, pigs, owls and cats) are in the Owl and Pussycat poem, and helped me slowly construct a story through the hints and clues Lear left. Names of places. Lake Pipple-popple and The City of Tosh I found in his admirable prose writings. Some of his drawings I simply had to include in the story. This walking fish is irresistible.

Or how about the wonderfully snooty Ostrich with its kinky boots?

For me the project really came alive when we put the animated film idea to one side and decided to create the story as a book. Dove Books made us an offer I couldn't refuse (since it involved no money!) and finally with the assistance of a wonderful illustrator (Wessla Weller) creating many wonderful line drawings inspired by Lear we have a book.

I have read the story for Dove Audio and in May John Du Prez and I recorded fifteen or so songs for this taped version. So if you have kids or know kids, or are renting some kids for a little while, or you just like Nonsense then I would be happy to send you a copy of either the book, or the tape, or the special edition of both if you would prefer.
